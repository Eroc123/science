B2.2 Movement in and out of the cell

Completed and reviewedLearnt

Diffusion - The net movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration down a concentration gradient, as a result of their random movement

Substances move in and out of cells through diffusion, water moves in and out through osmosis.

Osmosis - The net movement of water molecules from a region of high water potential (dilute solution) to a region of low water potential (concentrated solution) through a partially permeable membrane.

High solute = low water potential (less water molecules as its displaced by solute)

Low solute = high water potential (more water molecules as less displacement by solute)

Hypotonic solution - Osmosis in this is at a higher rate. lower mineral concentration, high water potential, causes animal cells to swell and become lyzed.  Causes plant cells to become turgid. The cell wall of plants can resist turgor pressure and allows for rigidity within the plant structure. Ideal for plants.

Hypertonic solution - Osmosis outwards is at a higher rate. higher solute concentration, low water potential, causes animal cells to shrink and shrivel, causes plant cells to become plasmolyzed - when the cell membrane detaches from the cell wall.

Isotonic solution - Osmosis in and out have an equal rate. Same mineral concentration and water potential as cell, causes animal cells to be normal, and causes plant cells to become flaccid. Flaccid plant cells cause the plant structure to become weak due to loss of rigidity and causes plants to wilt.  

Diffusion is affected by:

  • Temperature (higher temp = more diffusion due to higher kinetic energy of the particles allowing for faster movement and therefore faster diffusion)
  • Diffusion distance (higher distance = slower diffusion, since more time is needed for particles to spread out completely throughout the substance)
  • Concentration (higher concentration = higher diffusion, more particles with random movement, causing a overall faster rate of movement to the region with low concentration) Particles bump into each other more and are forced through the membrane
  • Surface area to volume ratio - higher surface area allows more particles to diffuse through at once. Higher surface area to volume ratio allows for faster rate of diffusion into and throughout the substance

Turgor pressure - The pressure applied by excess cell sap on the cell wall

(plant cell only, animal cells just burst)

Plants drink water through osmosis as the water particles move up the plant due to osmosis

The importance of water potential and osmosis in the uptake of water by plants

  • It is important that the water potential is higher outside the plant cells than inside, so that osmosis of water into the plant cells can occur. This increases the turgor pressure within the plant cell, increases the pressure between the cells and allows the plant tissue to maintain its rigidity.
  • Lower water potential within the plant allows for osmosis of water (and dissolved minerals) to occur into the roots.  

Also important in transport of water in plants

The importance of water potential and osmosis on animal cells and tissues

  • Water and dissolved minerals diffuse into the animal cell via osmosis and bring energy, nutrients and minerals to the cells.

B4 Enzymes


B4.1 Enzymes

Completed and reviewedLearnt

Enzymes are proteins that function as biological catalysts. They are folded into complex shapes to allow smaller molecules called substrates to fit into them. The result product is called a product.

How does temperature affect enzyme activity? 

  • A higher temperature allows for increased enzyme activity.
  • This is because of increased kinetic energy that both the enzymes and the substrates have. This allows for a higher net total of successful collisions.
  • Only applies up to a temperature called the ideal temperature (optimum temperature).
  • Beyond that, the enzymes start to get denatured, so even if the number collisions increase, the number of successful reactions decrease and enzyme activity drops rapidly.
  • When they denature, the active site changes shape

As temperature increases, enzyme activity increases gradually up until the ideal temperature. Beyond that, enzyme activity drops rapidly as they get denatured

How does pH affect enzyme activity? 

  • Enzyme activity is highest when the pH is in their ideal range.
  • Enzyme activity increases gradually as the pH increases, until their ideal pH is reached. Beyond that, enzyme activity gradually decreases.
  • Outside the ideal pH range, the enzyme’s active sites change shape and decreases the number of successful collisions
  • Extreme pH’s causes the enzymes to become denatured

The shape of the enzyme active site matches that of the substrate (key and lock theory)

Active site - portion of the enzyme that has a specific shape that latches onto the substrate

Substrate - The reactant that fits into the active site.

Denature - The permanent deformation of an enzyme’s active site such that it can no longer complement its substrate.

The substrate attaches to an enzyme’s active site. The active site weakens the bonds within the substrate and causes it to split into products.

Chlorophyll test and its necessity

  1.  Destarch a plant with variegated leaves for 24 hours (leaves with green and white areas)
  2. Move the plant into a warm and sunny area
  3. Test one of the leaves for starch using iodine

The areas with purple/black colour will coincide with the green parts of the leaf (with chlorophyll, where starch was produced) and the areas that are yellow/brown will coincide with the white areas of the leaf (without chlorophyll, where no starch was produced).

Carbon Dioxide test and its necessity

  1. Destarch a plant with green leaves for 24 hours
  2. Put the destarched leaf into a sealed flask with lime water on the bottom (lime water absorbs carbon dioxide) Leaf should still be attached to the plant
  3. Ensure that no air enters the flask. Leave the plant and flask in a sunny location for 48 hours.
  4. Carry out the starch test on the leaf in the flask.

The leaf inside the flask should not produce any starch (the entire leaf will be brown/yellow) whilst the outside leaves should have starch and be purple/black.

Necessity of light for photosynthesis

  1. Destarch a plant with green leaves for 24 hours
  2. Use paper clips to fix aluminium foil onto a part of the leaf. Leaf should still be attached
  3. Leave the plant in a sunny place for 48 hours
  4. Remove the aluminium foil and test the leaf for starch

The part of the leaf covered by aluminium foil should not have any starch (yellow/brown) whilst the part of the leaf left uncovered will have starch (purple/black)

Changes of states:

Freezing - Change of state from liquid to solid (losing energy). The particles start to move more and more slowly until the strengthened forces of attraction force them into fixed positions.

Melting - Change of state from solid to liquid (gaining energy). The particles start to move more and more quickly until they weaken the forces of attraction and lose the regular shape, instead now flowing more freely.

Condensing - Change of state from gas to liquid (losing energy). The particles start to move more and more slowly until the forces of attraction force them into droplets of liquid that cant move freely.

Boiling - Change of state from liquid to gas (gaining energy). The particles start to gain more and more energy until they overcome the forces of attraction and become fast flying and freely moving particles.

Sublimation - Change of state directly from gas to solid or solid to gas. The particles gain enough energy to completely overcome the forces of attraction to become gas or lose enough energy to be forced into completely fixed positions.

General Purity

Pure substance - A substance made of one type of element, compound or molecule.

Impure substance - A substance made of more than one type of element, compound or molecule that are not chemically bonded in any way.

Pure substances have fixed melting and boiling points.

Impurities will cause the melting point to decrease (depression in melting point) and the boiling point to increase (elevation in boiling point).

Impure substances have a range of boiling points and melting points (no fixed temperature at which it melts at).

Importance of purity:

Manufacturing of compounds for use in drugs and food additives.

Paper Chromatography

Paper chromatography - The use of paper to separate different substances based on their ability to dissolve in a solvent (how far they can migrate) and adsorb onto paper. (Invented by Mikhail Tswett in 1901) Explained further down.

Adsorption - When atoms, ions or molecules from a gas, liquid or dissolved solid adhese to a solid surface.

Adsorbate -  The substance that adhese to the solid surface

Adsorbent - The solid surface that the adsorbate will adhese to.

Chromatography requires a:

  • Stationary phase (Static part, solid or liquid)
  • Mobile phase (Moving part - Liquid or gas moving up the stationary phase)

C2.3 Methods of purification

Completed and reviewedLearnt

Filtration:

  • This method involves pushing a solution through a filter medium, where the holes are small enough to trap solute on one side of the filter but large enough to allow the fluid to pass through.
  • The mixture is thus separated and the solvent is purified (Called the filtrate)
  • Criteria for using this method of purification:
  • Dissolved substances can still pass through the filter and require other methods of purification to separate.
  • Three stages of filtration:
  • Filtration (mixture goes through filter)
  • Washing (filter is washed to clear the solute that has been trapped behind the filter medium)
  • Drying
  • Used in:
  • Airconditioners
  • Kidneys
  • Water purification
  • Vacuum pump
  • Air and oil filters of vehicles
  • Chemistry labs and industries
  • Waste water treatment plants

Crystallisation:

  • This method involves using the fact that crystalline solids have a very ordered structure and do not allow any foreign particles to be incorporated.
  • Crystallization steps:
  1. A solvent is chosen to separate the mixture. Requirements:
  1. The solvent must not react with the mixture
  2. The impurities should either be very soluble at room temperature (to make sure that the impurities dont crystallize with the substance being purified) or not soluble at boiling point (so that it can be filtered out)
  3. The solvent must be volatile (so it can be removed after the substance being purified has crystallized )
  1. The solution must be supersaturated with the mixture
  2. If the mixture contains impurities that are insoluble in the hot solvent, they should be filtered out.
  3. The solution is cooled, which causes the substance being purified to crystallise into a highly pure crystal, whilst the impure substance (if easily soluble at room temp) to remain dissolved.
  4. The crystals are then dried and separated from the solvent, which leaves very pure crystals.

Example:

Simple Distillation - Separating substances with vastly different boiling points by heating the impure substance.

  • This method relies on heating the mixture and then collecting the vapours of the substance with the lower boiling point. Since only the substance with the lower boiling point boils, the condensed vapours are thus purified.
  • Distillation Steps:
  1. A mixture of substances is chosen. The substances to be separated must have large differences in boiling points.
  2. The mixture is heated until it reaches the boiling point of the substance with the lower boiling point.
  3. The vapours of the substance with the lower boiling point is collected and condensed. The substance that has been separated from the rest of the mixture is highly purified.

Fractional Distillation - Separating liquids with different but similar boiling points by heating the impure substance.

  • This method relies on heating the mixture and then having the vapours rise into a fractional column. This fractional column has a temperature gradient that decreases as one goes higher.
  • Fractional Distillation Steps:
  1. A mixture is chosen. The substances must differ in boiling points but it does not have to be by a large margin.
  2. The mixture is heated until it starts to boil.
  3. The vapours then rise into a fractional column, which has a temperature gradient. The top is cooler than the bottom of the fractional column, which means that the higher the boiling point of the substance, the lower down the substance will condense.
  4. The substance with the higher boiling point will repeatedly evaporate and recondense at the same height on the fractional column
  5. The substance with the lower boiling point will rise to the top of the fractional column where it can be tapped out and condensed into a purified liquid.

P3.2 Pressure changes

Gases, as fast moving free particles, constantly bombard onto each other and walls. The bombardment of gas particles against a wall creates a force called pressure.

  • The smaller the volume of the container, the more frequent the collisions are with the walls, increasing the force of the gas
  • The larger the volume of gas, the more particles there are to collide with the wall, increasing pressure
  • The higher the temperature of the gas, the higher the pressure since the particles have more energy and collide more frequently with the wall.

P3.4 Measurement of temperature

Completed and reviewed

Physical properties such as thermal expansion that varies with temperature can be used to measure temperatures, such as in mercury or alcohol thermometers. Thermal expansion causes the liquid to rise up a glass tube with temperature markings. Potential difference is also affected by temperature (voltage)

Fixed points such as melting (0 degrees celsius) and boiling (100 degrees celsius) points of water are needed to appropriately calibrate thermometers and ensure that they are measuring temperature accurately according to the scale they were built for.

Bulb - provides the necessary liquid needed for thermal expansion to have a considerable effect on the height of the liquid going up the thermometer.

Scale and markings - allow the temperature to be read

Capillary tube - mercury or alcohol is forced into this tube when expanding to show temperature

Sensitivity - How much the thermometer responds to a temperature change and how readable the change is. A thermometer with high sensitivity can respond to fraction of a degree changes in temperature, which can be displayed with accurately spread out markings on the glass tube. Making the capillary tube more narrow means that a smaller temperature rise forces mercury/alcohol much higher up the thermometer

Range - The difference between the highest and lowest value readable by the thermometer. The higher the range, the higher the difference between the highest and lowest markings. Widening the tube increases the range, since it takes larger temperature differences for the thermal expansion of the liquid to rise the same height.

Linearity - The consistency of the thermal expansion. An inconsistent thermal expansion means that the liquid expands at a faster rate at certain temperatures. This means that the liquid can rise different amounts of each degree of temperature, causing inaccuracies in the markings.

Thermocouple

Two dissimilar metal wires are connected to a hot junction and a cold junction, with a voltmeter connected to the circuit. As the junction where the metals meet are heated, it produces an electrical current which varies with the temperature and can be read by the voltmeter as volts. The volts are then converted to temperature using a chart. It is very heat resistant and responds quickly to temperature changes, as metals are very conductive.

  1. The more dense fluid (colder) falls down, where they get pushed back over the heat source by other falling fluid masses.
